Question 1: Describe your machine learning pipeline. Produce a diagram of it to guide the reader (e.g. see Rybner et al 2022 Vocal markers of autism: Assessing the generalizability of ML models), and describe the different parts: data budgeting, data preprocessing, model choice and training, assessment of performance.

    

                ​​​​​​​    ​​​​​​​    ​​​​​​​    ​​​​​​​    ​​​​​

Data Budgeting:

For our data budgeting, we divided our datasets along the traditional 80/20 split for training and testing respectively. The empirical data was stratified along the variables before the split to match participants in the training and test set. On the simulated dataset, the only demographic variable we needed to match was diagnosis.

Feature Selection:

In a dataset with a large number of variables, such as our empirical dataset, it is important to only select relevant predictors firstly, to save on computational cost and secondly, because including too many predictors worsens performance and can lead to overfitting. We compared three methods: the lasso regression, which minimises the cost function, principal component analysis, which ranks the variables by magnitude and removing highly correlated values.


Preprocessing:

We scaled all our numeric variables onto a standard normal distribution. 

Model fitting:

We selected a logistic regression as our classification algorithm and set weakly informative priors. We then fit the regression on our training set and performed convergence tests and prior-posterior update checks to see if the chain converged correctly and if our priors were adequate.

Performance assessment:

We assessed cross-validation based on the f1 measure and on the area under the ROC.


Question 2: Briefly justify and describe your use of simulated data, and results from the pipeline on them.

In the context of this assignment, the use of fabricated data served multiple purposes: By generating data based on known "true values", we were able to check if our results fit with those values. Making a dataset that was structurally similar, but slimmer than the empirical data, allowed us to perfect our pipeline with lower computational expense. Additionally, by adding noise variables, we were able to check if our feature selection process succeeded in excluding these variables.
